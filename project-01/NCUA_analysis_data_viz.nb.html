And with over 2,300 columns (almost all named in the same format), it can be easy to mix up the columns.

head(acctnames[,1:2])

Before we do any analysis I want to update these column names to be more useful. The acctname dataframe contains the Account Numbers and Names for each field, I intend to use this to update the column names in the ncua_df dataframe.

temp_df <- ncua_df

names(temp_df)<-acctnames$AcctName[match(toupper(names(ncua_df)), toupper(acctnames$Account))]

names(temp_df)[is.na(names(temp_df))]<-names(ncua_df)[is.na(names(temp_df))]

ncua_df <- temp_df

rm(temp_df)

The code above will replace the Account Numbers with names, it might not be the most efficent way, but it works and it quick.

Use of Shares in Lending

To better show the relationship between deposits and loans we can do a scatterplot.

ggplot(ncua_df, aes(x=`Total Amount of Shares and Deposits`, y=`Total amount of Loans and Leases`))+
  geom_point()+
  labs(title="Loans of Credit Unions by State", caption = "NCUA 5300 Report (June 2021)", x="", y="")+
  guides(fill = FALSE)+
  theme(plot.title.position = "plot")+
  scale_y_log10(labels = scales::dollar)+
  scale_x_log10(labels = scales::dollar)

We can see in the plot above that almost all the deposits a credit union has is being actively used in Loans. It would be worth while to explore this ratio is greater detail.

sum(ncua_df$`Total amount of Loans and Leases`)/sum(ncua_df$`Total Amount of Shares and Deposits`)

We can see that as a total for the nation 69.5% of all Credit Union Deposits are currently being used in loans, but the plot above also showed that this ratio increased with total deposit amount which I further wanted to explore.

ggplot(ncua_df, aes(x=`Total Amount of Shares and Deposits`, y=`Total amount of Loans and Leases`/`Total Amount of Shares and Deposits`))+
  geom_point(size = 0.5, color = "Blue")+
  geom_smooth(method=lm, color = "Black")+
  geom_hline(yintercept = 1, color="Red")+
  labs(title="Relationship of Deposits to Loans", caption = "NCUA 5300 Report (June 2021)", x="Deposits", y="Percent of Deposits Currently on Loan")+
  guides(fill = FALSE)+
  theme(plot.title.position = "plot")+
  scale_y_continuous(labels = scales::percent)+
  scale_x_log10(labels = scales::dollar)+
  coord_cartesian(ylim = c(0,1))

The plot above again shows the increasing trend of percent of shares currently on Loan as a Credit Unions total deposits increases. This makes some sense as a Credit Unions cost to operate on a per deposit basis should decrease as the credit union increases in size. Meaning per member the cost to operate a large credit union should be smaller than the cost to operate a small credit union, we can confirm this as well using this dataset.

Operating Expenses per Dollar of Deposit

In an attempt to show the increased operating efficiency of credit unions with increased deposits I decided to take a look at Non-Interest Expenses. These would be expenses such as Employee Compensation, Office Occupancy Expenses and Promotional Expenses. Essentially these would be expenses not paid to the members in the form of dividends and instead serve to allow the operation of the credit union.

As a quick graph, lets see what the makeup of Non-Interest Expenses are nationally

expenses <- ncua_df %>%
  select (`Employee Compensation and Benefits`,`Office Occupancy Expense`,`Office Operations Expense`,`Educational and Promotional Expense`,`Loan Servicing Expense`,`Professional and Outside Services`,`Miscellaneous Operating Expenses`) %>%
  colSums() %>%
  data.frame()

names(expenses)<-"YTD Amount"
expenses$Category <- row.names(expenses)
row.names(expenses) <- NULL

expenses<-arrange(expenses,desc(`YTD Amount`))

total <- sum(expenses$`YTD Amount`)

ggplot(data = expenses, aes(x="", y=`YTD Amount`, fill=Category))+
  geom_bar(stat="identity", width=1) +
  coord_polar("y", start=0)+
  theme_void()+
  geom_text(aes(label = paste0(round(`YTD Amount`/total,2)*100, "%")), position = position_stack(vjust=0.5)) +
  labs(x = NULL, y = NULL, fill = NULL)

We can see Employee Compensation and Benefits make up the majority of non-interest expenses for Credit Unions Nationally, with Office Operations Expenses coming in second.

I am surprised that office operations is higher than office occupancy.

With the concept of Non-Interest Expenses established, we can now look at efficiency.

There are a few ways of measuring efficiency and the NCUA has a few standards such as an efficiency ratio which is a measure of income over expenses, but for my purpose I want to see efficiency from a members point of view. As previously mentioned I did this by comparing a credit unions total deposits to their non-interest expenses.

ggplot(ncua_df, aes(x=`Total Amount of Shares and Deposits`, y=`Total Non-Interest Expense`))+
  geom_point(size = 0.5, color = "Blue")+
  geom_smooth(method=lm, color = "Black")+
  labs(title="Relationship of Non-Interest Expenses to Deposits", caption = "NCUA 5300 Report (June 2021)", x="Deposits", y="Non-Interest Expenses YTD")+
  guides(fill = FALSE)+
  theme(plot.title.position = "plot")+
  scale_y_log10(labels = scales::dollar)+
  scale_x_log10(labels = scales::dollar)

The graph above allows us to see a few outliers which have <$10,000 in deposits. So for the next graph I have removed these.

ggplot(filter(ncua_df,`Total Amount of Shares and Deposits`>10000), aes(x=`Total Amount of Shares and Deposits`, y=`Total Non-Interest Expense`/`Total Amount of Shares and Deposits`))+
  geom_point(size = 0.5, color = "Blue")+
  geom_smooth(method=lm, color = "Black")+
  labs(title="Relationship of Non-Interest Expenses to Deposits", caption = "NCUA 5300 Report (June 2021)", x="Deposits", y="Operating Effiency per Dollar of Deposit")+
  guides(fill = FALSE)+
  theme(plot.title.position = "plot")+
  scale_y_continuous(labels = scales::dollar)+
  scale_x_log10(labels = scales::dollar)+
  coord_cartesian(ylim = c(0,0.05))

The graph above shows what I had expected. As a Credit Unions total deposits increases their non-interest income as a factor of total deposits decreases. This would prove that larger credit unions tend to be more efficient.
